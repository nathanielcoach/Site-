What This Actually Is

Coaches say they hate business and sales. They don't. They hate the approach. They hate the scripts and the funnels and the "overcome their objections" stuff. Take all that away and what's left? Talking to people you care about and offering to help. That's not sales. That's just being a person.

But first — you have to stop coaching people before you're in a session. You're doing it at dinner. You're doing it in the DMs. You're doing it at the grocery store with someone who didn't ask. There's no container. There's no professionalism. You're not in your office. Nobody gave you permission. And then you wonder why it doesn't land. Coaching without a container isn't coaching. It's just unsolicited advice from someone who read a book.

Save it for the session.

And find the easiest way in. You don't have to cold message strangers. You don't have to build a funnel. The easiest path is the one that's already in front of you. The friend who keeps venting to you about the same thing. The coworker who said "I wish I had someone to talk to." The person who already asked for your help once and you didn't charge them. Start there.

The kind of coaching you do is a path too. Stop picking a niche off a list. What do people already come to you for? What conversations do you keep having without trying? That's your coaching. You don't have to invent it. You just have to notice it.

And your structure can be as simple as you need it to be. "I meet with people on Zoom for an hour. It's $200." That's a coaching practice. You don't need a website. You don't need a logo. You don't need a niche statement. You need a time, a price, and someone to say it to. We help you figure out all three.

Six months with a group of coaches who all want the same thing: enough clients to stop panicking, a practice that doesn't make them want to nap, and people around them who understand what this job is like.

It's six months because you need reps.

Every day you take a small risk. Not a big one. Not "quit your job and go all in." A micro risk. Something scary enough to make your stomach flip but not so scary you won't do it. Message that person. Say what you do out loud. Ask one more question than you normally would. And then you'll notice something — the person you talked to was fine with it. They didn't run. They didn't judge you. They were fine. So tomorrow you take a slightly bigger risk. And the day after that. Six months of that and the stuff that used to terrify you is just normal.

What can I do today that's scary enough to grow but not so scary I won't do it?

That's the daily question. And after six months of asking it, you're not just a better coach. You're more yourself around more people. You feel good about yourself. Not in a fake affirmation way. In a "I did something hard today and it went fine" way.

This is supposed to be fun.

High engagement. Low attachment.

Care about people. Be interested in them. Ask questions like you mean it. But stop white-knuckling the outcome. You can be all in on a conversation and completely detached from whether it turns into a client. That's the sweet spot. Most coaches are either checked out or desperate. We teach you the middle.

Dumb assumptive questions are fun. That's when you guess something about somebody. It doesn't have to be right. It just has to be playful. You're on a connection call. They're telling you about their week. You say "Let me guess — you've been thinking about this since Tuesday and you almost cancelled this call three times."

They laugh. "How did you know?" Now they're not nervous anymore. Now they're telling you what's really going on. Not the polished version. The real one. All because you guessed something dumb and got close enough to make them laugh. That's a micro risk. And nobody talks to people like that on these calls. Which is exactly why it works.

And you need to get good at follow-through. Following up. Checking in. Staying in touch with people without it feeling weird. You get needy when you don't know what to say next. Fix that and everything gets lighter.

The Two Moves

There are only two moves as a coach. You're either asking a question or you're expressing yourself. That's it. Get good at those two things and everything else falls into place.

Asking questions: when you're with someone — ask them about who they are. Not who they think you want them to be. Who they are. Ask so many questions that the act falls apart. People lie to themselves about how much they want this. They say "maybe coaching isn't for me" and "I should probably get a real job" and "who am I to charge people for this." They talk themselves out of it before anyone else gets the chance to. Your job is to ask enough questions that the real person shows up. Where coaching starts. Where connection starts.

Everything else is just small talk in a costume.

Expressing yourself: say what you see. Say what you feel. Say the thing you're afraid to say. Not a script. Not a coaching question you read in a book. You — the person — saying something honest to another person. That's what makes people trust you. That's what makes people hire you. Not your credentials. Not your website. You, being honest out loud.

Want to know if someone is interested in coaching? It's not a mystery. People tell you when they want help. They lean in. They ask follow-up questions. They say "how does that work?" They keep the conversation going when they could have walked away. You just have to know what to listen for.

But you have to take the lead.

Most coaches wait. They're on their phone scrolling through nothing. Not working. Not reaching out. Not even thinking about coaching. Just... scrolling.

Because distractions feel safer than solutions.

Lead the conversation. Lead the session. Lead the relationship. Your clients hired you because they need someone willing to go where they won't go alone.

If you're waiting for them to lead, who's coaching who?

Inviting. Not pitching. Not selling. Inviting.

An invitation doesn't feel needy. It sounds like "I think I could help you with that — want to talk about it?" It sounds like "I'm starting something in April and you came to mind." A person who believes in what they do and isn't afraid to say so. You practice this until you stop rehearsing it in the shower and start saying it to actual humans.

Three skills. Most programs teach one. Maybe two.

Coaching skills, business skills, and people skills. This one teaches all three. And then gives you six months to practice them while two coaches watch your back.

Stop wasting your chances with people who want help. They're right in front of you. They're telling you their problems. They're asking you questions. And you're freezing, or changing the subject, or waiting for a better moment that never comes.

Follow the values. The Gremlin gets quieter.

You get values to follow. Not rules. Values. And when you follow those values — when you practice being light, being curious, being brave, showing up — you become the coach you've been pretending you're not ready to be.

The Stuff Nobody Teaches You

What do you talk about when you get on a call with someone? Not a sales pitch — a real conversation. You talk about them. You ask what they want and what keeps getting in the way. You ask what they've tried and why it didn't work. You ask what scares them. You ask until you understand who they are. And then — if coaching makes sense — you say so. If it doesn't, you say that too. Two people talking where one of them happens to be a coach.

Everyone has walls up. That's where you come in.

Your job is to be so honest and so patient that the walls come down on their own. And they will. If you let them.

But you can't do any of this sitting on your couch waiting for clients to find you. They won't. Take charge. Reach out to people. Start conversations. Follow up with the ones you already talked to. Make offers to the ones who are ready. The coach with people in their DMs who already said "tell me more" — people they haven't replied to in weeks? That coach isn't bad at marketing. They're bad at replying. Coaches who move — even scared, even messy, even before they feel ready — build practices.

So move.

The vibe. You know the one.

The vibe where people tell you things. Where they say the thing they haven't said to anyone. Where they skip the small talk and go straight to what matters. You can learn how to do that. You learn it by slowing down and paying attention. When you create that vibe, people start telling you what they need. Coaching starts there.

You know you've created it when a prospect leaves you a 20-minute voicemail. Not a polite "hey, call me back." Twenty minutes of them telling you what's going on. What they're afraid of. What they want. What they've tried. They didn't plan to talk that long. They just felt safe enough to keep going. That's not an accident. That's a skill. What Coaching Actually Looks Like

Coaches don't want to ruffle feathers. That's the problem. They'd rather have a nice conversation where everyone feels comfortable and nothing changes than say the thing that might make someone squirm. You can want people to relax around you and still not be attached to whether they do. Say what needs to be said. Let them have whatever reaction they're going to have. That's their business.

People hide things when they think you're going to judge them. Or get mad at them. Clients do it. Prospects do it. Everyone does it. They tell you the version of the story that makes them look good. They leave out the embarrassing part. They skip the thing they're ashamed of. And you can't coach somebody on the stuff they're hiding from you. So you have to make it safe enough that they stop hiding. Not by being soft. By being the kind of person who doesn't flinch.

Which means you have to learn to not judge your clients. And not get angry with them. Even when they do the thing you told them not to do. Even when they lie to you. Even when they ghost you for two weeks and come back like nothing happened. Your reaction is the game. If they think you're disappointed in them, they'll hide more. If they think you're mad, they'll shut down. Stay curious. Stay light. That's how people open up.

But don't confuse being light with being their buddy. Coaches put themselves in the friend zone when they don't bring in any professionalism. No structure. No boundaries. No clear start or end to the session. Just two friends talking. Your friend who owns a bakery — you buy brownies from her. Your friend who's a massage therapist — you book appointments. You pay them because they created something separate from the friendship. Something with a time, a place, and a price. If your coaching looks exactly like hanging out, nobody knows when to pay you. Create the distinction and people will.

You're going to learn to pry.

Coaches are too polite. They walk on eggshells with their clients. They ask one question and accept the first answer. But the first answer is almost never the truth. The truth is three questions deeper.

Prying isn't rude. Prying is care.

It means you're not willing to let someone get away with the watered-down version of their life. You push — with love — until the truth shows up.

But you also learn when to let up. Sometimes people need a second. They just said something big and they need to breathe. So you let them breathe. You crack a joke. You say "that was a lot, huh." And then — when they're ready — you go back in. Other times they're dodging and you know it. That's when you push back. Not mean. Just honest. "I don't think that's what's going on. What's really going on?" Knowing when to push and when to back off — that's the part nobody teaches you. We do.

That's the job. The rest is just getting in the room.

Talk to people like a human. Not like a coach who read a book about connecting. Look them in the eye. Listen to what they're saying. Respond to what they mean, not just what they said. Laugh when something's funny. Be quiet when something's heavy. Let people breathe. Let people think. Let people feel something before you jump in with a coaching question.

You won't find it in a definition. You'll find it in the room.

What is a good coach? A good coach treats every single person like they are their best friend. Not nice-nice. Not professional-warm. Actually caring. About the person in front of you. About what they want. About what's stopping them. About their life. When you treat someone like your best friend, you tell them the truth. You root for them. You don't let them off the hook. You remember what they said last week. You ask how that thing went.

That's a good coach. And you already are one. You just haven't let yourself act like it yet.

Be honest. And when they're honest back — tell them. "That took guts to say." "I'm glad you told me that." "The realest thing you've said in three sessions."

You go first. That's it.

You go first. They open up. You go deeper. They go deeper. And before you know it you're in a conversation that changes somebody's life. Practice that until you stop thinking about it.

Each one stole from a different character. On purpose.

Each month, we teach one method. Each method is named after a character — from a movie, a TV show, or in one case, a real woman who teaches kindergarten in Sedona. Because stories stick better than frameworks. And characters are easier to remember than acronyms.
